This is the note for some phonetics, to help memorization. Please note that this note contains the phonetics of consonants and vowels, but leaves out the articulatory processes.

1.1  Voiceless and Voiced

The concept of voicing is simple.

Voiceless: Air passes through the glottis freely, without vibration.
Voiced: Air passing through the glottis makes the vocal folds vibrate.

1.3  Manners of Articulation

Manners of articulation refers to how would the air flows through the oral cavity.

1.3.1  Stops

Stops are consonants produced by completely blocking the airflow in the oral cavity. (cannot continue)

Oral Stops block airflow from passing through the nasal cavity.

BilabialAlveolarVelarGlottal
Voiceless/p/ pit/t/ tall/k/ curl/ʔ/ uh-oh

Nasal Stops allow airflow to pass through the nasal cavity.

1.3.2  Fricatives

Fricatives are consonant sounds produced with continuous airflow through a narrow constriction.

1.3.3  Affricates

Affricates are produced by first making a complete closure in the oral cavity and then releasing it into a fricative-like constriction.

1.3.4  Approximants (Liquids)

Lateral Approximants: produced by placing the tongue tip against the alveolar ridge while allowing air to flow along the sides of the tongue.

Retroflex Approximants: produced either by bunching the tongue near the hard palate or by curling the tongue tip toward the alveolar ridge.

Alveolar
Voiced, Lateral/l/ lead
Voiced, Retroflex/r/ reed

Glides are produced with only slight constriction in the oral cavity.

2  Vowels

Vowel can be classified by height, backness, tenseness, roundedness.

It is important to learn the pronunciation of these IPA symbols.

FrontCentralBack
High[i] sheep[u] pool
[ɪ] ship[ʊ] pull
Mid[ej] bait[ə] famous[ow] boat
[ɛ] bet[ʌ] but[ɔj] boy
[ɔ] more
Low[æ] bat[aɪ] buy[a] spa
[aw] how

Vowel height refers to the height of the tongue relative to its position in the mouth. This is the vertical movement of the tongue. (high, mid, low)

Vowel backness is the horizontal movement of the tongue in the mouth. We have to consider how far forward or backward the tongue is with respect to its resting position. (front, central, back)

Vowel rounding refers to the protrusion (rounding) of the lips as you produce a vowel. Vowels produced with protruded lips are referred to as rounded, while vowels without lip rounding are called unrounded. Only the high back and mid back consonants are considered rounded in Canadian English.

Vowel tenseness characterizes vowels into tense vowels and lax vowels. Tense vowels (as in English [i] ‘seat’ and [u] ‘suit’) have tighter constriction of the vocal tract and are typically longer in duration. Lax vowels (as in English [ɪ] ‘sit’ and [ʊ] ‘put’) can be produced with the same tongue position as their tense counterparts, but with less constriction. English monosyllabic words usually end in tense vowels
